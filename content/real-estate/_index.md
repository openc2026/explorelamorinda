## Buying in Lamorinda

Buying in Lamorinda is not like buying in most of America. Three things make it unusual: the schools (which drive demand into very specific boundary lines), the fire-zone designations (which affect insurance, inspections, and disclosures), and the speed of the market (which routinely produces multiple-offer situations on quality homes).

A typical Lamorinda buying process looks like this:

### 1. Get Pre-Approved Before You Look

Lamorinda buyers compete in a market where cash offers and waived contingencies are common. A solid pre-approval letter from a reputable local lender is the absolute minimum. A pre-underwritten approval is even better. Sellers and listing agents read pre-approval letters carefully — a letter from a lender they don't know will be viewed more skeptically than one from a long-time East Bay lender.

### 2. Decide on Town and School Zone First

This is the most important decision you'll make, and most buyers underweight it. Before falling in love with any specific house, decide: Lafayette, Moraga, or Orinda? Then within that, which K-8 school boundary? A house that's not in the school zone you want is a house that may cost you a major life decision later. See the [Lamorinda Schools Guide](/schools/) and the individual town pages ([Lafayette](/lafayette/), [Moraga](/moraga/), [Orinda](/orinda/)) for the full breakdown.

### 3. Understand Fire-Zone Considerations

Most of Lamorinda's hillside areas fall in California's State Responsibility Area (SRA) and Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zone (VHFHSZ). What this means in practice:

- **Insurance** — Homeowner's insurance is more expensive and harder to obtain in these zones. Get a quote *before* removing contingencies. The California FAIR Plan is increasingly the backstop for hillside properties.
- **Defensible space** — State law requires 100 feet of cleared defensible space around any structure in fire zones. Inspect for compliance during your due diligence.
- **Roof and vent materials** — Class-A roofs and ember-resistant vents are now required for new construction and substantial remodels.
- **Disclosures** — Sellers must disclose hazard-zone status; read these carefully.

None of this should stop you from buying a hillside home — most Lamorinda residents live in fire-zone areas and have for decades. It just means you go in with eyes open.

### 4. Inspections Matter More, Not Less, in a Hot Market

In multiple-offer situations, buyers often consider waiving inspection contingencies. This is risky in Lamorinda, where older homes (1950s–1970s ranch and split-level are common) frequently have specific known issues: original galvanized plumbing, knob-and-tube electrical, foundation movement on hillside lots, deferred deck work, and the occasional surprise underground oil tank. The right move is usually to do inspections *before* writing the offer, so you can write a more competitive offer with confidence.

Common Lamorinda inspection categories:

- General home inspection
- Pest inspection (termites and dry rot are the East Bay reality)
- Sewer lateral inspection (Contra Costa is a Lateral Compliance Certificate region)
- Roof inspection (especially on hillside or wooded lots)
- Foundation / structural specialist (on hillside lots)
- Pool inspection (where applicable)
- Chimney inspection (older homes)

### 5. Write a Competitive Offer

The Lamorinda offer game has its own rhythm. Highly competitive offers typically include:

- A pre-approval letter from a reputable lender (ideally local)
- Significant earnest money (1–3% common)
- Short or removed inspection contingency (only if you've pre-inspected)
- A meaningful down payment (20%+ is normal; cash offers do happen)
- A personal letter where local culture and the seller permit it (regulations vary)
- Flexibility on closing date and possession

A good local agent will read the listing agent and the seller's preferences and tune your offer accordingly.

### 6. Escrow and Closing

Lamorinda escrows typically run 21–30 days. Most title and escrow work is handled by a small set of East Bay companies that have been in the area for decades. Closing costs are split per California convention; expect transfer tax, title insurance, escrow fees, and prorated property tax adjustments.

## Selling in Lamorinda

Selling in Lamorinda has its own playbook. The biggest mistake sellers make is treating their Lamorinda home like a generic Bay Area listing. The buyers here are sophisticated and the comparable set is small and well-watched. Specifics matter.

### 1. Timing — When to List

Spring (March through May) is historically the strongest Lamorinda selling window — buyer families are aligning to school-year moves, the gardens are at their best, and weekend foot traffic is highest. A second strong window opens late August through October. The two slowest months are typically late December and early January.

This doesn't mean off-season listings don't work — they do, especially for unique properties — but in a typical sale, listing in the strong season can mean a meaningful price premium and a faster close.

### 2. Prep — Where the Money Goes

Lamorinda buyers expect a high standard of presentation. The high-leverage prep items, in rough order of ROI:

- **Paint** — Fresh interior paint in neutrals; touch up exterior. The single highest ROI prep item.
- **Floors** — Refinish hardwood; replace carpets that show wear.
- **Landscaping and curb appeal** — A polished front yard pays back disproportionately. Bay Area buyers walk up the path before they see any interior.
- **Kitchens and baths** — Light updates (countertops, hardware, fixtures, lighting) often outperform full remodels at sale time.
- **Decks and outdoor space** — Lamorinda is sold partly on outdoor living. Repair the deck, stain the fence, clean and stage the patio.
- **Roof, exterior siding, gutters** — Anything that shows during a walk-up will get noted in inspections; address it ahead of time.
- **Staging** — Professional staging is the norm for Lamorinda listings. Empty homes typically sell for less.

Pre-listing inspections (general, pest, roof, sewer lateral) are increasingly standard in the Lamorinda market. They give you control of the narrative and reduce buyer surprises later.

### 3. Pricing — Where Sellers Get It Wrong

The temptation to over-price is real, especially after a few years of strong appreciation. The Lamorinda market punishes over-pricing more harshly than it rewards aspirational numbers. Properties that sit on market for 30+ days typically end up selling below where a well-priced launch would have landed. The right strategy is usually to price at or slightly below market and let competition drive the result.

### 4. Choosing Your Listing Agent

For a high-value Lamorinda sale, agent selection matters more than for most transactions. Look for:

- A demonstrated track record of recent Lamorinda sales (not just generic East Bay)
- Specific neighborhood knowledge — they should be able to talk about your specific street
- A real marketing plan, including photography, video, online presence, and broker outreach
- Honest pricing guidance backed by current comparable sales
- A network of trusted vendors (stagers, painters, inspectors, repair contractors)

### 5. Disclosures and Compliance

California sellers carry meaningful disclosure obligations: the Transfer Disclosure Statement (TDS), Seller Property Questionnaire (SPQ), and Natural Hazard Disclosure (NHD) for fire, flood, earthquake, and other zones. Contra Costa County also requires a sewer lateral inspection or compliance certificate before close in most cases. A good listing agent will guide you through this.

## Lamorinda Market at a Glance

Lamorinda's residential market is small but liquid — a few hundred single-family transactions per year across the three towns. Compared to broader Bay Area trends, the Lamorinda market tends to:

- **Be more price-resilient** in downturns than urban San Francisco — school-driven demand provides a floor.
- **Move first in upswings**, especially in spring buying seasons, driven by tight inventory and school timing.
- **Show wider per-square-foot variance** than most markets, because school boundaries, hillside vs. flat lots, and BART proximity all matter so much.
